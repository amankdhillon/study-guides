Course Overview & Big Data Concepts

CSE 190: Algorithms and Systems for Biomolecular Big Data. Knowledge bases, real-world data challenges, and the MassIVE platform.

Real World Big Data vs. Traditional ML

Traditional ML: input dataset + labels, output model predicting labels from data. Real world big data is different:

May have millions or billions of labels. Data is not fully labeled. Labels can be related (parent/child/sibling) or hierarchical. Full set of labels is not known in advance. Unclear how to model matches for never-seen data vectors and labels. Data grows and accumulates every day.

Knowledge Bases (KB)

Key Definition

Knowledge bases capture knowledge (data/label associations) derived from, and supported by, data. Associated with the union of all datasets.

Reference knowledge: defines the representative data vector for a label (e.g., photo on an ID card).

Detection knowledge: establishes the observation of a data vector/label in a given dataset.

KB error control questions: How to determine the representative for a label? How to control KB error rates (when first determined and over time)? How to use the KB to improve models for labeling new data (considering both matching error and KB error)?

MassIVE & Global Data

MassIVE = Mass Spectrometry Interactive Virtual Environment (massive.ucsd.edu). Repository: >20,000 datasets, 10s of billions of data points. Algorithmic analysis: >100s of millions of identifications, >10 million labels.

MassIVE-KB: Millions of reference entries. International reference for global proteomics knowledge.

Mass Spectrometry Basics

MS is the main technology for high-throughput analysis of proteins and small molecules.

Proteins Run Life

Genes: Human ~20,000-22,000; Mouse ~20,000-22,000; Worm (C. Elegans) ~19,000; Rice/Corn ~32,000-45,000.

Each gene may generate several proteins via: combinatorial splicing, sequence variation, post-translational modifications (hundreds of types and sites), quantification/turnover, protein structure/interactions, endogenous and immune peptides (e.g., insulin).

Protein Sequence as Weighted Alphabet

An amino acid sequence can be modeled as a string over a weighted alphabet. Each amino acid has a specific mass.

Sequences of amino acids are almost equivalent to sequences of amino acid masses. Exception: m(I) = m(L) = 113.1 -- Isoleucine and Leucine have the same mass.

Parent mass m(p) of peptide p = a1,...,an is: m(p) = SUM of m(ai) for i=1..n

Peptide Mass (m/z) Calculations

How to calculate measured m/z at different charge states.

Core Formula

Measured m/z
m/z = (sum_of_AA_masses + 18.010565 + z * 1.007276) / z Integer form: m/z = (sum_of_AA_masses + 18 + z * 1) / z

Slides Example: PEPTIDE

P(97) + E(129) + P(97) + T(101) + I(113) + D(115) + E(129) = 781

z=1: (781 + 18 + 1) / 1 = 800 m/z

z=2: (781 + 18 + 2) / 2 = 400.5 m/z

Why include H2O? ▶
Amino acid residue masses represent the mass after losing water during peptide bond formation. The intact peptide retains one H2O for the N-terminus H and C-terminus OH. So you add it back once for the whole peptide.
Why multiply z charges? ▶
MS measures ionized molecules. Each charge = one added proton (1.007276 Da). If charge is z, the peptide gained z protons. The instrument measures total mass (including protons) divided by z.
What about fragment ion m/z at charge >1? ▶
For a fragment ion at charge z: m/z = (fragment_neutral_mass + z * 1.007276) / z. For example, a doubly-charged y8 ion: y8++ m/z = (suffix_AA_mass + 18.010565 + 2 * 1.007276) / 2. This is important for Assignment 2 where you calculate y8++ for VQPTESIVR.

Tandem Mass Spectrometry (MS/MS)

MS/MS fragments peptides and measures the resulting pieces, creating prefix and suffix masses that encode the sequence.

How MS/MS Works

A selected peptide ion is fragmented at different backbone positions, producing:

Prefix fragments (N-terminus side) → generate b-ions and a-ions

Suffix fragments (C-terminus side) → generate y-ions

For peptide LARGE, prefix masses are: L, LA, LAR, LARG -- cumulative sums from the N-terminus.

Modifications in MS/MS

A modification changes the mass at a specific site. For modified peptide LARG*E, prefix masses shift at and after the modification. This creates mass shifts in the spectrum.

Example from slides: Mass(F) = 147 Da. If a modification of -10.059 occurs: 147 - 10 = 137 = mass(H). This is how MODa discovers modifications.

Good vs. Poor PSMs

Good PSM Features

High percentage of total intensity explained by peptide ions at low mass tolerance. Consecutive series of y fragments (sometimes also b ions). Known sequence-specific fragmentation patterns (e.g., high-intensity peaks before/after Proline).

Poor PSM Indicators

Insufficient matched peaks (ID based on absence of better alternatives). Many unexplained peaks. Low explained intensity. Low signal-to-noise ratio.

Examples from slides: HDSKWFKEPYFVHAVEWGSHVYFFFR (insufficient matched peaks). FTASAGIQVVGDDLTVTNPKR (many unexplained peaks, low SNR).

Understanding the Offsets

How Offsets Work

Offsets are relative to the prefix residue mass (sum of AA masses for a prefix) or suffix residue mass.

b-ion = prefix_residue_mass + 1.0073 (sum of AA masses + mass of hydrogen ion)

y-ion = suffix_residue_mass + 18.0106 (H2O) + 1.0073 (proton) = suffix_residue_mass + 19.0178

a-ion = b-ion - CO = b-ion - 27.9949 = prefix_residue_mass - 26.9876 (offset -27 integer)

Notation: b and y Ion Numbering

For a peptide of length n: b1 is the first prefix (1 AA from N-terminus), b2 is two AAs, etc. y1 is the last single AA (from C-terminus), y2 is the last two, etc.

So for PEPTIDE (length 7): b1=P, b2=PE, b3=PEP, b4=PEPT, b5=PEPTI, b6=PEPTID, b7=PEPTIDE (full). y1=E, y2=DE, y3=IDE, y4=TIDE, y5=PTIDE, y6=EPTIDE, y7=PEPTIDE.

Complementary Ions

How to find the mass of a complementary y-ion given a b-ion mass, and vice versa.

The Complementary Ion Formula

Key Formula
For complementary ions: mass(b-ion) + mass(y-ion) = M + 1 Therefore: mass(y-ion) = M + 1 - mass(b-ion) mass(b-ion) = M + 1 - mass(y-ion) Where M = parent mass (measured at charge 1)

Example: PEPTIDE (M = 800)

Prefix PEP: sum_AA = 323. b-ion = 324.

Complementary suffix TIDE: sum_AA = 458. y-ion = 477.

Check: 324 + 477 = 801 = M + 1 = 800 + 1.

Vertex Score Computation

For a spectrum peak at mass m with parent mass M:

1. Add scores from all prefix ions around mass m.

2. Find complementary y-ion at my = M - m + 1.

3. Add scores from all suffix ions around mass my.

All prefix/suffix ions found at relative mass offsets from the main b/y-ions.

Database Search Overview

Finding a peptide from the database with maximal match to an experimental spectrum.

Problem Definition

Database Search Problem

Input: S (experimental spectrum), D (database of protein sequences), Delta (set of possible ion types), m (parent mass).

Output: A peptide of mass m from D whose theoretical spectrum matches S the best.

Pipeline

Proteins → generate peptides in silico (enzyme rules) → filter by parent mass → generate theoretical spectra → score against experimental spectrum.

Maestro: Stratified Search

1. MS-Cluster: Clusters spectra from same peptide. Reduces redundancy in input data.

2. M-SPLIT (Spectral Library): Matches to MassIVE-KB. Smallest search space, most information. Quality evaluated by Cosine(M,A).

3. MS-GF+ (Database Search): Matches to reference proteome sequences. Quality evaluated by SpecProb (p-value) and DB E-Value. Discovers new peptides not in KB.

4. MODa (Blind/Open Search): Allows K mass offsets from database sequences. Largest search space. Only for otherwise-unidentified spectra. Discovers novel modifications.

Mass Filtering

Only peptides with mass within tolerance of the experimental parent mass are scored. This dramatically reduces search space. Many sequences may share a common mass; sequences of same mass may score differently.

Protein Digestion & Peptide Types

Enzyme cleavage rules and peptide classification.

Enzyme Rules

TrypsinCleaves to the RIGHT of R, K -- if NOT followed by P
Asp-NCleaves to the LEFT of D, E
ChymotrypsinCleaves to the RIGHT of F, Y, W, L -- if NOT followed by P

Peptide Classification (Trypsin)

Definitions

Tryptic (fully-tryptic): Both preceded by K/R (or protein start) AND ending in K/R (or protein end).

Semi-tryptic: Preceded by K/R OR ending in K/R (but not both).

Non-tryptic: Neither preceded by K/R nor ending in K/R.

How to find all fully-tryptic peptides from a protein?▶

1. Scan the sequence left to right. Mark every position after R or K (unless followed by P) as a cleavage site. Also consider the protein start and end.

2. Each segment between two consecutive cleavage sites is one fully-tryptic peptide (with 0 missed cleavages).

3. For missed cleavages: a peptide with m missed cleavages spans m+1 consecutive tryptic segments.

Example from Assignment 1, protein P69905: MVLSPADKTNVKAAWGK... -- the first cleavage after K at position 8 (since next AA is T, not P), giving first tryptic peptide MVLSPADK.

Peptide-Spectrum Matches & Quality

How spectrum peaks are annotated with peptide fragment ions.

PSM Annotation

Prefix fragments generate b ions (a-ions also often prominent). Suffix fragments generate y ions.

Ions must be charged to be detectable. Can be multiply-charged. 13C isotopes help determine fragment charges.

Quality Metrics

For M-SPLIT (spectral library): Cosine(M,A) -- cosine similarity between query and library spectrum.

For MS-GF+: SpecProb (p-value of match among all possible peptides) and DB E-Value (expected number of matches in the reference proteome database).

Likelihood Scoring

The core scoring model: probability ratios for signal vs. noise.

Likelihood Model Basics

Core Concept

Likelihood = Prob(signal) / Prob(noise).

A spectrum with 1000 mass bins and 100 peaks has Prob(noise) ~ 0.1.

Gain (Peak Observed)

Gain = Prob(signal) / Prob(noise) Example: b-ion frequency = 0.5, Prob(noise) = 0.1 Gain = 0.5 / 0.1 = 5 "Observing a b-ion is 5 times more common than noise."

Loss (Peak Missing)

Loss = (1 - Prob(signal)) / (1 - Prob(noise)) Example: b-ion not observed Loss = (1 - 0.5) / (1 - 0.1) = 0.5 / 0.9 = 0.55
Key Intuition

Higher Prob(signal) → smaller numerator → harsher penalty for missing it.

Higher Prob(noise) → smaller denominator → looser penalty (less contrast with noise: lower gains for seeing a peak AND lower penalties for missing it).

Combining Multiple Fragments

Multiple evidence combined as product of ratios. Positive evidence (detection) increases score (ratios >1). Negative evidence (missing) decreases score (ratios 0-1).

Slides example: Observed: b, b-13C, a, b-H2O, a-H2O. Missed: b-NH3, a-NH3.

Product observed: 5 x 1.1 x 1.7 x 1.2 x 1.5 = ~16.83

Product missed: 0.94 x 0.99 = ~0.93

Total: ~15.66

Whole Peptide Score

For PEPTIDE, the total score considers all prefix/suffix at each breakpoint:

Score(P) x Score(EPTIDE) x Score(PE) x Score(PTIDE) x ... x Score(PEPTID) x Score(E)

Each "Score" includes both prefix and suffix fragment evidence at that breakpoint.

Log-Likelihood Tables

Log transforms simplify products to sums. Critical These exact values are used in scoring calculations.

Properties of Log

log(a x b) = log(a) + log(b) -- products become sums.

log(ratio > 1) is positive -- premium for positive evidence.

log(ratio between 0 and 1) is negative -- penalty for missing evidence.

Scoring Walkthrough

Step-by-step procedure for calculating the match score at a prefix mass. This is the exact procedure for Assignment 2 scoring questions.

Scoring a Prefix Mass (Prefix Ions Only)

1
Identify the prefix mass you are scoring (e.g., 525.23 from Assignment 2).
2
For each prefix ion type, calculate expected mass using integer offsets:
b-ion: prefix_mass + 1 b-13C: prefix_mass + 2 b-NH3: prefix_mass - 16 b-H2O: prefix_mass - 17 a-ion: prefix_mass - 27 a-NH3: prefix_mass - 44 a-H2O: prefix_mass - 45
3
Check each expected mass against the experimental spectrum. A peak is "detected" if any experimental peak falls within 0.5 Da of the expected mass (tolerance from Assignment 2). You can use the interactive spectrum viewer "Ions" panel to help identify matches.
4
Look up the score for each ion type: if peak found, add Log(Gain). If peak NOT found, add Log(Loss).
5
Sum all values. This is the prefix-only log-likelihood score for that prefix mass.

Adding Suffix Ions (Full Score)

6
Find the complementary suffix residue mass. The b-ion for this prefix is at prefix_mass + 1. The complementary y-ion mass = M + 1 - b_ion_mass = M - prefix_mass. The suffix residue mass = y_ion_mass - 19 = M - prefix_mass - 19.
7
For each suffix ion type, calculate expected mass:
y-ion: suffix_residue_mass + 19 y-13C: suffix_residue_mass + 20 y-NH3: suffix_residue_mass + 2 y-H2O: suffix_residue_mass + 1
8
Check peaks and look up scores just as for prefix ions.
9
Total score = sum of all prefix log-scores + sum of all suffix log-scores.

Quick Reference: What to Add

Peak found at expected mass → add the positive Log(Gain) value.

Peak NOT found → add the negative Log(Loss) value.

The total will be positive if there is good evidence, negative or near-zero if poor match.

Spectrum Graphs & De Novo Sequencing

Determining the amino acid sequence directly from the spectrum without a database.

Why De Novo?

Many sequences are missing from databases. Biology creates new sequences every day (mutation, antibodies, immune peptides). Exhaustive enumeration is impractical: most spectra consider at least 10^9 possible sequences, some go up to >10^20.

Spectrum Graph Definition

Definition (from slides)

For spectrum of mass M, charge 1:

1. Define vertex v_m for every peak at mass m. Score(v_m) = intensity at mass m.

2. Add v_1 for b-ion zero (start). Add v_P for last b-ion at mass M - 18 (end of peptide).

3. Edge from v_j to v_i if i - j is an amino acid mass.

Dynamic Programming Sequencing

AminoAcid(m) = the amino acid whose mass is m.

BestPath(v_i) = Score(v_i) + max over all predecessors v_j of BestPath(v_j)

where v_j iterates over predecessors such that i - j is a valid amino acid mass.

Using v_k as highest-scoring predecessor: Label(v_i) = AminoAcid(i - k)

Sequence reconstructed backwards from v_P.

Limitations

Missing peaks → incomplete reconstructions, mass gaps with many possible sequences.

Noise peaks can make incorrect sequences score higher.

Ambiguity between b and y ions (no way to know in advance).

Specific: y1 for E = 129+19 = 148; b1 for F = 147+1 = 148. These are indistinguishable.

Proline (P, mass 97): if b1 for P is at mass 98, it might be missing from the spectrum.

Sequence Tags

An adaptation: extract subsequences only from high-intensity/high-scoring regions, avoiding uncertain regions. Paths can start and end at any vertex.

De Novo Algorithm & Spreadsheet

Step-by-step procedure for the spreadsheet-based de novo sequencing, as in Assignment 2.

Y-Ion Series Algorithm (Slide 10, Lectures 6-7)

Information Columns

Peak mass: measured mass of each peak.

Peak intensity: height of each peak.

Amino acid predecessors: For each peak of mass P, list (A, M) tuples where mass(A) = P - M and M is the mass of a predecessor peak.

Algorithm Columns

Y-ion path to end: Does this peak connect (via some path of amino acid mass differences) to the y-ion mass for the total peptide mass? (mass 800 for PEPTIDE example).

Path score: Best y-ion path score up to this peak.

Path sequence: Amino acid sequence of the highest-scoring path up to this peak.

Step-by-Step

1
List all peaks sorted by mass. Include the start vertex and end vertex (the y-ion mass corresponding to total peptide mass).
2
For each peak at mass P, find predecessors: peaks at mass M where P - M = valid amino acid mass.
3
Check "path to end": work backwards from the end vertex. A peak has a path to end if it IS the end, or if any of its successors (peaks reached by adding an amino acid mass) have a path to end.
4
Calculate path score: BestPath(v_i) = Score(v_i) + max(BestPath(predecessors that connect to start)).
5
Track the sequence: at each node, record which amino acid was used from the best predecessor.
6
Final: the sequence and score at the end vertex = the de novo result.

Missing Peaks Solution

Add vertices at every integer mass (score = 0 for masses with no peak). This allows paths through unobserved masses. Defines M vertices for a spectrum of mass M.

Converting Y-ions to B-ion Masses

If spectrum parent mass = M (charge 1): peptide mass = M - 18 - 1.

Matching y-ion for a b-ion at mass m: y-ion mass = M + 1 - m.

Matching b-ion for a y-ion at mass m: b-ion mass = M + 1 - m.

Counting Peptides & P-values

Using dynamic programming to count possible peptides and calculate statistical significance.

NumPaths Recursion

ConnectToStart(v) = true if v = v_1, or if any predecessor v_j has ConnectToStart(v_j) = true.

NumPaths(v_i): If v_i = v_1 then 1. Else: SUM of NumPaths(v_j) over valid predecessors with ConnectToStart = true.

Counts(m, t) Matrix

For a peptide of mass M matched to Boolean spectrum B (B[m] = 1 if peak at mass m, 0 otherwise):

Counts(m, t) = SUM over amino acid masses a of: Counts(m - a, t - B[m]) Counts(m, t) = number of sequences of mass m matching exactly t peaks. Last column = histogram of how many sequences match for each number of peaks.

With Intensity/Likelihood Scores

Counts(m, t) = SUM over a of: Counts(m - a, t - S[m]) where S[m] = intensity at mass m

With Amino Acid Probabilities

Not all amino acids equally common. Humans cannot synthesize 9 essential AAs: F, V, T, W, M, L, I, K, H.

Counts(m, t) = SUM over a of: Prob(a) x Counts(m - a, t - S[m])

Counts(M, T) = probability that a random peptide of mass M matches with score T.

P-value

P-value Definition
p-value = Prob(score(R) >= T) = SUM of Counts(M, t) for all t >= T

Expected false matches in database: p x N (N = database peptides matching parent mass).

Prob of at least one false match: 1 - (1 - p)^N.

Peptide of mass 12 Da, Boolean spectrum B, only two amino acid masses: 2 Da and 3 Da.

Formula for cell: IF(condition, formula_if_true, formula_if_false)

With probabilities: Prob(mass=2) = 0.6, Prob(mass=3) = 0.4. Define named ranges "Prob2" and "Prob3" in spreadsheet.

False Discovery Rates (FDR)

Assessing reliability of identifications with target-decoy strategies.

Standard Definitions

True Positive (TP): Sample peptide generated the spectrum AND algorithm identifies it.

False Positive (FP): Algorithm identifies peptide NOT in the sample.

True Negative (TN): Peptide NOT in sample, NOT identified.

False Negative (FN): Peptide IS in sample but NOT identified.

FPR vs. FDR

Critical Distinction
False Positive Rate = FP / (FP + TN) Uses distribution of incorrect scores (negative, from decoy). Equivalent to p-value in the distribution of negative scores. False Discovery Rate = FP / (FP + TP) Percentage of decoy matches above the score threshold.

Target-Decoy Strategy

Null hypothesis: Each spectrum is generated by a random (peptide-like) amino acid sequence.

Practical FDR: FDR = (# decoy hits) / (# target hits).

Three Levels of FDR

Spectrum-level FDR: Each spectrum = one observation. Error rate does NOT transfer to higher levels.

Peptide-level FDR: Each peptide assigned best-scoring spectrum's score. Multiple spectra for same peptide reinforce consistency, not correctness.

Protein-level FDR: Combines peptide scores (e.g., sum of scores of uniquely-mapped peptides). Complicated by peptide sharing across proteins.

How to compute spectrum-level FDR from a PSM list?▶

Sort all PSMs by score (descending). At each score threshold, count decoy hits and target hits above threshold. FDR = (# decoy) / (# target). Number of IDs at FDR < X% = number of target hits at threshold where FDR first drops below X%.

Peptide-level FDR: how is it different?▶

For each unique peptide sequence, keep only the ONE best score among all its PSMs. Apply FDR on this reduced list. Stricter because you lose credit for multiple spectra of the same peptide.

Protein-level FDR: how to calculate?▶

Calculate protein scores as sum of matched peptide scores (uniquely mapped). Apply decoy-based FDR on protein scores. You may need to satisfy BOTH protein FDR < X% AND peptide FDR < X% simultaneously.

Protein Inference & Parsimony

Going from peptide identifications to protein identifications.

Why Peptides are Shared

Duplication, fusion, loss of N-terminus, divergent paralogs cause peptides to map to multiple proteins. Protein-peptide assignment is complicated by isoforms (different versions of same gene), paralogs (related genes in same species), orthologs (matching gene in other species).

Protein Contextual Types

Definitions

HasUniqueMatch: At least one peptide matched uniquely to this protein only.

Indiscernible: Two or more proteins match exactly the same set of peptides.

Subset: All peptides of protein A are a subset of protein B's peptides.

Subsumable: All peptides for a protein also support other proteins (complex overlap).

Maximum Parsimony (Greedy)

1
Select all proteins with uniquely-matched peptides.
2
Discard subset and subsumable proteins.
3
Greedy set cover: select protein matching most remaining peptides, assign those peptides. Repeat until all peptides assigned.

Set Cover Problem

Input: N proteins (each = set of matched peptides). Output: K ≤ N proteins whose union covers all peptides. Objective: minimize K.

NP-Hard. Greedy returns at most K x log(N) sets. Generally accepted in proteomics.

Database Choice Impact

SwissProt reviewed: 20,381. With isoforms: 42,426. Full reference: 93,588.

Larger databases find more "proteins" but most additional ones come from mapping same peptides to isoforms. Only a small number of unique peptides distinguish true isoforms. Proteins can change names between database versions.

MassIVE Website Guide

How to navigate MassIVE for assignments and quizzes. Step-by-step instructions for every major action.

Viewing Search Results

1
M-SPLIT results (spectral library search): Use the Cosine(M,A) column to evaluate match quality. Click the spectrum icon at the far left of each row to inspect individual spectra.
2
MS-GF+ results (database search): Use SpecProb and DB E-Value columns to evaluate quality. SpecProb = p-value of match among all possible peptides. DB E-Value = expected number of matches in the reference proteome. Click the Lorikeet visualization panel to inspect matches.
3
MODa results (open/blind search): Sequences show modifications like (F,-10.059). Click "View in MassIVE" then "Search MassIVE-KB reference spectra".

Filtering Results Tables

1
Find the filter box for the column you want (e.g., "Peptide" column).
2
Enter your search term (e.g., a peptide sequence like LSKEEIER).
3
Click the "Apply filters" button on the top-left of the table.
4
You can filter by any column: Charge, Protein, Peptide, etc.

Inspecting a Spectrum

1
Click the spectrum icon on the far left of any result row.
2
The interactive viewer opens showing the annotated MS/MS spectrum.
3
Use the "Ions" panel (top-right of the interactive spectrum viewer) to label peaks matching expected ion masses. This is critical for Assignment 2 -- it helps you see which fragment ions are present.
4
Look for: consecutive y-ion or b-ion series, high explained intensity, absence of many unexplained peaks, high-intensity peaks near Proline residues.

Comparing Spectra via USI

1
Copy the USI for your experimental spectrum from the results view.
2
Go to MassIVE-KB and search for the reference spectrum for the same peptide. You can also use the reference spectra links provided in assignments.
3
Open the Spectrum Identifier Resolver and paste both USIs into the two input fields.
4
Click "Reset Figure" (if needed). The tool shows a mirror plot with shared and unique peaks, plus a cosine score quantifying similarity.
5
For modified peptides from MODa: if the peptide has a mass offset, scroll to the bottom of the Resolver page to change parameters that compensate for the mass difference. Then scroll back up for updated results.

Evaluating Match Quality (Assignment-Style)

Qualitative Assessment

Describe what you see: Are most major peaks explained? Is there a consecutive ion series? Are there many unexplained high-intensity peaks? How does the spectrum visually compare between your experimental spectrum and the MassIVE-KB reference?

Quantitative Assessment

Use USI cosines from the Spectrum Identifier Resolver. Higher cosine = better match. Compare the cosine of your experimental spectrum vs. reference against the cosine of alternative peptide matches vs. their references. The peptide whose experimental-to-reference cosine is highest is the best match.

Peptide Mass Calculations

Given peptide VQPTESIVR from SARS-CoV-2 Spike glycoprotein, what is the m/z at charge 2?▶

Step 1: Sum the amino acid masses (monoisotopic from course table):

V(99.06841) + Q(128.05858) + P(97.05276) + T(101.04768) + E(129.04259) + S(87.03203) + I(113.08406) + V(99.06841) + R(156.10111) = sum

Step 2: Apply formula: m/z = (sum + 18.010565 + 2 x 1.007276) / 2

Use the Calculators page to verify your answer.

How do you calculate b4+ (singly-charged) for VQPTESIVR?▶

b4 = prefix of first 4 amino acids = VQPT.

Sum = V(99.06841) + Q(128.05858) + P(97.05276) + T(101.04768) = prefix_sum

b4+ m/z = (prefix_sum + 1 x 1.007276) / 1 = prefix_sum + 1.007276

How do you calculate y8++ (doubly-charged) for VQPTESIVR?▶

y8 = suffix of last 8 amino acids. VQPTESIVR has 9 AAs, so y8 = QPTESIVR (everything except the first V).

suffix_sum = Q(128.05858) + P(97.05276) + T(101.04768) + E(129.04259) + S(87.03203) + I(113.08406) + V(99.06841) + R(156.10111)

y8++ m/z = (suffix_sum + 18.010565 + 2 x 1.007276) / 2

Tryptic Digestion

How to find all fully-tryptic peptides from a protein?▶

Step 1: Scan left to right. Mark every position after R or K, UNLESS the next amino acid is P. The protein start and protein end are also boundaries.

Step 2: Each segment between consecutive boundaries = one fully-tryptic peptide (0 missed cleavages).

Example (P69905): MVLSPADKTNVKAAWGKVGAHAGEYGAEALERMFLSFPTTK...

K at position 8 (next is T, not P) → cleavage. First peptide: MVLSPADK.

R at position 32 (check next AA) → potential cleavage. Continue through entire sequence.

Missed cleavages: A peptide with 1 missed cleavage spans 2 consecutive tryptic segments. E.g., MVLSPADKTNVK has 1 missed cleavage (skips the K at position 8).

What is a semi-tryptic peptide with zero missed cleavages?▶

A semi-tryptic peptide has ONE tryptic end and ONE non-tryptic end, with no internal missed cleavage sites.

Example: If MVLSPADK is a tryptic peptide, then VLSPADK would be semi-tryptic (starts at V, which is not preceded by K/R, but ends at K). It has zero missed cleavages because there are no K/R-not-followed-by-P sites internal to VLSPADK.

Log-Likelihood Scoring

Calculate the match score for a prefix mass using only prefix (a/b) ions.▶

Step 1: For prefix mass M, calculate 7 expected masses:

b: M+1, b-13C: M+2, b-NH3: M-16, b-H2O: M-17, a: M-27, a-NH3: M-44, a-H2O: M-45

Step 2: For each, check if a peak exists within 0.5 Da in the spectrum (use the "Ions" panel in the interactive viewer).

Step 3: Sum the log-likelihood values:

Detected → b: +2.33, b13C: +0.14, bNH3: +0.58, bH2O: +0.26, a: +0.77, aNH3: +0.14, aH2O: +0.58

Missing → b: -0.85, b13C: -0.02, bNH3: -0.08, bH2O: -0.03, a: -0.12, aNH3: -0.02, aH2O: -0.08

The sum = your prefix-only score.

Now add suffix (y) ions to get the combined score.▶

Step 1: Find complementary suffix. b-ion = prefix_mass + 1. y-ion = M + 1 - b-ion = M - prefix_mass. Suffix residue mass = y-ion - 19.

Step 2: Calculate 4 expected suffix masses from suffix_residue_mass:

y: +19, y-13C: +20, y-NH3: +2, y-H2O: +1

Step 3: Check peaks, sum log values:

Detected → y: +2.38, y13C: +0.68, yNH3: +0.14, yH2O: +0.49

Missing → y: -0.91, y13C: -0.10, yNH3: -0.02, yH2O: -0.07

Total = prefix score + suffix score.

Unit Mass Error

How to calculate unit mass error for a PSM?▶

Step 1: Calculate theoretical m/z = (sum_AA_masses + 18.010565 + z x 1.007276) / z

Step 2: Unit mass error = experimental m/z - theoretical m/z (in Da).

Example from Assignment 1: VDPVNFKLLSH matched to spectrum at m/z 1270, charge 1. Compute sum of AA masses, apply formula, subtract from 1270.

FDR Calculations

Given a PSM list with target/decoy hits, how to compute FDR at each level?▶

Spectrum-level: Sort all PSMs by score (descending). At each threshold: FDR = #decoy / #target. Count target IDs where FDR < your cutoff (e.g., 10%).

Peptide-level: For each unique peptide sequence, keep only the ONE best score. Re-sort. Apply same FDR calculation on this reduced list.

Protein-level: Calculate protein scores as sum of scores of matched peptides (mapped uniquely). Sort proteins by score. Apply FDR on protein scores.

Combined: You may need to satisfy BOTH protein FDR < 10% AND peptide FDR < 10% simultaneously. The number of protein IDs meeting both criteria may be less than meeting either alone.

Protein Inference

How to classify proteins as HasUniqueMatch, Indiscernible, Subset, or Subsumable?▶

HasUniqueMatch: At least one peptide maps ONLY to this protein and no other.

Indiscernible: Two or more proteins share the EXACT same set of matched peptides -- they cannot be distinguished.

Subset: All peptides of protein A are also matched to protein B, but B has additional peptides that A does not.

Subsumable: Every peptide of protein A is shared with at least one other protein, but no single other protein contains ALL of A's peptides. This is a complex overlap situation.

To classify: list all peptides for each protein/isoform, compare the sets, and apply definitions.

De Novo Sequencing

How to fill in the de novo sequencing spreadsheet?▶

Step 1: List all spectrum peaks sorted by mass. Note their intensities.

Step 2: For each peak P, find predecessors: peaks at mass M where P - M equals a valid amino acid mass (check the AA integer mass table: 57, 71, 87, 97, 99, 101, 103, 113, 114, 115, 128, 129, 131, 137, 147, 156, 163, 186).

Step 3: Determine "path to end": work backwards from the end vertex. A peak has path to end if it IS the end vertex, or if any successor peak (reached by adding an AA mass) has path to end.

Step 4: Dynamic programming path score: for each peak, BestScore = peak_intensity + max(BestScore of valid predecessors that connect to start).

Step 5: Track sequence: at each peak, record which AA edge gave the best score.

Step 6: Read final sequence from the end vertex by tracing back through the best predecessors.
